SubjectFreestanding cafeteria tables
Tablesn = 3
Study sitePulbit-maru (풀빛마루), Daejeon

Cafeteria table configurations are the set of spatial arrangements achievable with freestanding cafeteria tables. Although the number of theoretically possible configurations grows rapidly with table count, the observed configuration at the study site is the spaced I-configuration, in which the three tables stand aligned but separated by sizable gaps.[1] All joined configurations remain undocumented in everyday practice at this site.

This page constitutes a systematic survey of the configuration space for n = 3 tables, together with field documentation of configurations as they are tested in situ. As of June 2026, two configurations — the I-configuration (joined linear) and the L-configuration — have been field-tested.[2]

Configuration space (n = 3) [edit]

For three identical square tables on an orthogonal grid, the configuration space includes edge-joined configurations (tables share a full edge) and corner-adjacent configurations (tables share only a corner point). All orientations within a fixed room are treated as distinct entries. Configurations are listed with their field-verification status. The named classes are followed by the G-series: configurations generated by random placement of three tables on a 4 × 4 grid, deduplicated under translation, screened against the named classes (196 candidates survived), and reduced to a maximum-dissimilarity subset of 24, listed in decreasing order of similarity to the field-tested L-configuration.[original research?]

Field documentation [edit]

Experiment 1: L-configuration (June 2026)

The first field test of a non-standard configuration was conducted on 12 June 2026 at 13:10 KST at the Pulbit-maru (풀빛마루) cafeteria, Daejeon, at a corner site where three tables habitually stand in the spaced I-configuration.[2] No materials or permissions were required. The three tables were moved from their separated positions: two were aligned along the wall and the third was joined perpendicular at the outer end, producing a 90° interior corner.

Prior to this experiment, no instance of a person eating at an L-shaped cafeteria table arrangement had been observed by the investigator.[original research?]

  • The completed L-configuration viewed frontally. The 90° joint between the linear segment and the perpendicular table is visible at center.
  • The linear (wall-side) segment viewed from its terminus. Chairs remain in their pre-experiment positions.
  • Site context: the configuration in relation to the recycling station and service window, Pulbit-maru cafeteria.
